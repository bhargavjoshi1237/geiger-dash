Pricing and unit economics

Geiger Studio Pricing Strategy

Recommendation based on the 13 repositories under C:\Pro and provider pricing reviewed on June 13, 2026.

Executive Summary

  • Replace the current $99 / $149 / $249 structure with Free, Starter $29, Team $79, and Studio $179. Keep a Custom configurator at monthly pricing only. The lower entry point fits the suite's present maturity, while Team and Studio preserve room for support and future infrastructure.
  • Sell product access at the workspace level, not once per active project. Meter the resources that actually create cost: seats, projects, storage, AI spend, outbound messages, automation runs, signature requests, ticket transactions, and live-media minutes.
  • Do not promise unlimited AI, storage, campaign sending, video, or ticketing inside any fixed plan. These workloads have very different cost curves and can erase margin quickly. Bundle useful allowances, then charge transparent overages.
  • Use one central entitlement and usage model through Prisma, but keep large files out of Postgres. Store metadata and permissions in the central database; use object storage plus CDN delivery for user assets, uploads, media, exports, and canvas binaries.

The current pricing model does not match the suite

The public page prices 8 products, but the suite has 12 customer-facing products plus the dashboard shell. The missing products are Office, Docs, Events, and Campaign. At the same time, Flow contains a separate $10/$20 per-user mock plan, so there are already two incompatible pricing stories in the codebase.

Current behavior Why it is risky Recommended correction
Extra product price is multiplied by active projects. A workspace enabling one module across three projects can pay three times for the same access. That is hard to explain and unrelated to most infrastructure cost. Charge product access once per workspace. Charge projects separately only after the plan allowance.
"AI credits" and "automation runs" have no customer-visible definition. The customer cannot predict value, and the business cannot prove the allowance covers provider cost. Define every usage meter and show included quantity, current use, overage price, and spend cap.
The yearly multiplier discounts the whole estimate, including variable usage. Usage is not known a year in advance. Discounting overages weakens margin and complicates reconciliation. Discount only the fixed plan fee. Bill overages monthly on both monthly and annual subscriptions.
All products are treated as similar add-ons. Notes text, Assets media, Campaign email, Grey AI, and Chat video have radically different cost profiles. Bundle low-cost collaboration products; meter high-variance products by their cost driver.

Evidence: components/pricing/plan_cards.jsx, lib/product-routes.mjs, and Flow's upgrade dialog.

Four meters cover almost every product cost

The products share one commercial foundation, but they do not share one variable cost. The pricing system should classify every entitlement under one of four meters, then add product-specific meters only where necessary.

Cost family Products Primary meter Pricing treatment
Collaboration core Flow, Notes, Canvas, Forms, text Chat Seats, active projects, database records, realtime messages Bundle generously in plans. These create suite adoption and usually have low marginal cost under hard limits.
Files and delivery Assets, Office, Docs, Content, file attachments GB-month stored, transformed assets, CDN delivery Include a storage pool per workspace; charge storage overage and use fair-use delivery limits.
Outbound and transactions Campaign, Forms confirmations, Events, document signing Email/SMS sends, contacts, responses, registrations, envelopes, paid ticket value Bundle small allowances. Charge sends and signatures by usage; charge Events a platform fee plus payment processing.
Compute and media Grey, AI features, OCR, transcription, image/video processing, audio/video calls Provider cost, participant minutes, processed minutes/images Never unlimited. Use a prepaid usage balance, explicit overage, and workspace spend caps.

Code evidence includes Notes realtime collaboration and uploads, Canvas autosave with embedded binary state, Forms response storage, Office files and embedded media, Assets' DAM roadmap, Campaign sending workflows, Events ticketing, and Content personalization/edge delivery.

Gross-margin guardrail: keep expected monthly cost of service below about $7 for Starter, $20 for Team, and $45 for Studio. That targets roughly 75% gross margin before payment fees and creates room for support, failed jobs, abuse, backups, and future infrastructure.

Custom should be modular and monthly only

Custom is for customers who need an unusual product mix, not a discounted path around the fixed plans. Start it at $19/month for the shared workspace foundation, include three Core products at no additional charge, and make every configuration month-to-month.

Overages must follow the expensive action

Customers should pay more only when their use creates cost or material value. The first release can use simple blocks rather than a complex real-time bill.

Meter Suggested overage Rule

Automation runs$1.00 per 1,000Count a workflow execution, not every internal step, for simple customer billing.

Do not market "unlimited" before abuse controls exist. Every variable meter needs a soft warning, a customer-set spend cap, a hard service cap, and an internal kill switch.

Provider costs support this structure, not cost-plus pricing

The base platform is inexpensive when shared, but several workloads remain open-ended. Current official list pricing shows why fixed plans can have healthy margin while storage, AI, email, and live media still need controls.

Provider signal Current public price signal Pricing implication
Supabase Pro starts at $25; billing examples put a small always-on project near $30 after compute credit. Database, storage, egress, auth, realtime, and image transforms have separate quotas. One shared production project is affordable early, but usage must be attributed per workspace.
Vercel Pro starts at $20 before extra seats and usage; functions, transfer, image optimization, workflows, logs, and observability are metered. Cache public content and assets aggressively; do not use server rendering for every request by default.
Cloudflare R2 Standard storage is $0.015 per GB-month with free internet egress; operations are separately metered. Use R2 or equivalent object storage for large user files instead of Postgres JSON or database blobs.
Resend $20 includes 50,000 emails; additional email is listed at $0.90 per 1,000 on Pro. Email can be profitably bundled in moderate amounts, but Campaign needs send-based limits.
OpenAI API Model prices vary widely by model, context length, input, cached input, output, and service tier. AI must be tracked in provider-cost units, not a promise of unlimited actions.
Cloudflare RealtimeKit Audio/video participants are listed at $0.002 per participant-minute; recording and streaming exports cost more. Calls need participant-minute allowances and paid overage even if text Chat is included.

An early production baseline of roughly $75-$200/month before meaningful usage is a planning range, not a quote. It excludes labor, support, taxes, payment processing, security reviews, and enterprise compliance.

The central data model should own access and usage

Prisma should provide one commercial source of truth across all product deployments. Product apps can remain separate Next.js projects, but plan access and usage must not be hardcoded separately in each repository.

Important architecture correction: Canvas currently stores Excalidraw binary files inside JSONB, while Notes and Office already use storage buckets for media. Move binary payloads to object storage, retain only references and editor state in Postgres, and serve private files through short-lived signed URLs or an authenticated delivery service.

Recommended Next Steps

  1. Freeze the commercial vocabulary. Define workspace, member, guest, active project, storage GB-month, AI usage, automation run, outbound message, registration, and signature request.
  2. Create one versioned plan catalog in Prisma. Seed the proposed plans and products; make the pricing page read the catalog instead of exporting hardcoded arrays.
  3. Instrument usage before charging overages. Start with storage bytes, AI provider cost, email sends, automation runs, signatures, ticket transactions, and participant-minutes.
  4. Build the new pricing page in two layers. Show four simple fixed-plan cards first; place Custom below them with a monthly-only badge and a recommendation that points users back to Team or Studio when cheaper.
  5. Launch with warnings before automatic overage billing. For the first billing cycles, collect usage, alert at 70/90/100%, and manually review high-usage accounts. Turn on automatic overages after the cost model is validated.
  6. Review prices after 30-50 paying workspaces. Use actual gross margin, activation, conversion, support time, storage, AI cost, and product attachment rates rather than competitor pricing alone.

Questions that should be answered before launch

  • Is Geiger primarily sold to individuals, small teams, agencies, or internal company departments?
  • Will guests and external reviewers be free, limited, or counted as seats?
  • Which products are production-ready enough to be sold rather than shown as early access?
  • Will Campaign send from Geiger-managed domains, customer domains, or customer-owned providers?
  • Will Events collect ticket money as a platform through Stripe Connect, or will each organizer connect its own account?
  • Does Grey use one managed AI provider, a model router, or bring-your-own-key?
  • What data residency, retention, backup, and deletion promises will be made publicly?

Caveats and Assumptions

This recommendation is based on repository code, product research files, schemas, and public provider list prices. It does not use actual customer usage, invoices, support time, conversion data, or negotiated vendor discounts. Several products are currently prototypes, so future cost is inferred from the promised features rather than measured production traffic.

The proposed prices are launch hypotheses. They are designed to be understandable, leave margin for ordinary SaaS operation, and isolate open-ended workloads. They should be validated with real usage and willingness-to-pay interviews before being treated as permanent.

Prepared for Geiger Studio. No pricing-page implementation or billing behavior was changed as part of this analysis.
